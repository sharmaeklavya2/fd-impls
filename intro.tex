\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Our Contributions}

We study implications between fairness notions.
Formally, a fairness notion $F_1$ implies another notion $F_2$ if every $F_1$-fair allocation is also $F_2$-fair.
We present several implications and prove non-implications between fairness notions.
Some of these results are already known, some are non-trivial generalizations of existing results,
and some are completely new results.
These implication results help put fairness notions into a hierarchy.
See \cref{fig:additive-nny} for the implications we show among fairness notions
for additive goods and additive chores when agents have equal entitlements.

Many different settings of fair division have been considered in the literature:
goods vs chores vs mixed manna, equal vs unequal entitlements, and different types of valuation functions.
Special cases have also been studied, like identical valuations or fair division among just two agents.
We consider all the settings given by the combination of all these aspects of fair division.
At first, it would seem that this would lead to a combinatorial explosion of different settings.
However, we get around this by encoding our implication and non-implication results in a machine-readable format
and implementing an inference engine that uses these results to automatically deduce new results.

E.g., if you ask the inference engine ``Does epistemic envy-freeness (EEF) imply maximin share (MMS) for goods
with additive valuations?", it would give an affirmative answer based on these two results we show in the paper:
\begin{enumerate}
\item EEF implies proportionality (PROP) under subadditive valuations for mixed manna.
\item PROP implies MMS for superadditive valuations for mixed manna.
\end{enumerate}
We implement our inference engine in JavaScript as a client-side web application.
The inference engine is not tied to the specifics of fair division,
and so may be of independent interest.

Some fairness notions were originally defined only for very specific fair division settings.
E.g., EFX was defined in \cite{caragiannis2019unreasonable} for additive goods.
%(Although EFX was also defined for mixed manna by many subsequent works,
%there are now several competing definitions, and their relative merits are unclear.)
We extend all fairness notions to the most general fair division setting we consider:
mixed manna with unequal entitlements.
In some cases, picking an appropriate definition was non-trivial,
and we present several observations to motivate the definitions we propose.

\subsection{Structure of the Paper}

In \cref{sec:prelims}, we formally define the fair division problem and introduce associated notation.
In \cref{sec:settings}, we describe the different fair division settings we consider.
In \cref{sec:notions}, we describe all the fairness notions that we consider.
In \cref{sec:old-impls,sec:new-impls}, we present several implictions (and non-implications) between
fairness notions. \Cref{sec:old-impls} contains known results and their simple extensions.
\Cref{sec:new-impls} contains novel results.
In \cref{sec:cpig}, we describe our inference engine.
\Cref{sec:dags} contains a collection of inference DAGs, similar to \cref{fig:additive-nny},
for many different fair division settings.
